Prior to Customer Camp, she built and operated multiple businesses including an agency, a venture-backed startup, and a consulting practice.

---

# Video Summary

In this interview, Katelyn Bourgoin discusses Jobs To Be Done (JTBD), customer research, audience building, product creation, marketing strategy, and creator business models.

The core theme throughout the discussion is that customers do not buy products because of demographics or features. Instead, they buy products to make progress in their lives. Understanding that progress, the events that trigger buying decisions, and the struggles customers face is often the difference between successful marketing and ineffective marketing.

Katelyn argues that most companies spend too much time focusing on product features, assumptions, and internal opinions while spending too little time understanding real customer behavior through research and conversations.

# Key Takeaways

## 1. Customers Buy Progress, Not Products

One of the central principles of Jobs To Be Done is that customers hire products and services to help them make progress.

Instead of focusing on customer attributes such as:

- Age
- Gender
- Location
- Job title

Companies should focus on:

- What customers are trying to achieve
- What problems they are facing
- What triggered their search for a solution
- What alternatives they considered

### Key Quote

> "People seek out products because they're trying to make progress in their lives."

---

## 2. Most Companies Don't Truly Understand Their Customers

Many businesses struggle to clearly define their audience.

This lack of clarity leads to:

- Weak positioning
- Ineffective messaging
- Poor product decisions

Customer understanding should be built through research rather than assumptions.

---

## 3. Customer Interviews Are One Of The Most Valuable Marketing Tools

Customer interviews help uncover:

- Buying motivations
- Decision-making processes
- Pain points
- Alternative solutions considered
- Emotional drivers

The goal is not to ask customers what they think about your product.

The goal is to understand what was happening in their world before they started searching for a solution.

---

## 4. Trigger Events Start Buying Journeys

Customers rarely buy products spontaneously.

Examples of trigger events:

- Starting a business
- Changing jobs
- Having a child
- Hiring employees
- Experiencing a major problem

Understanding trigger events allows marketers to identify buyers earlier and create more relevant messaging.

---

## 5. The BarkBox Example Demonstrates How Buyers Really Think

The customer:

- Owned a young dog
- Had recently become a mother
- Felt guilty because she could no longer spend as much time with her dog

The real job she was trying to accomplish was:

> "Keep my dog happy and occupied while I'm busy."

This example demonstrates how customers often purchase products because of emotional circumstances rather than product specifications.

---

## 6. Features Matter Less Than Outcomes

Most marketers communicate features.

Customers care about outcomes.

**Feature:**

> Durable dog toys

**Outcome:**

> Hours of entertainment for your dog

Customers buy outcomes, not features.

---

## 7. Customer Stories Reveal Better Marketing Opportunities

Stories reveal:

- Emotions
- Motivations
- Struggles
- Desired outcomes

These stories often become the foundation for stronger messaging, positioning, and content.

---

## 8. Most Marketing Content Starts Too Late

Many companies focus their messaging on people who are already evaluating products.

Understanding trigger events allows marketers to:

- Build awareness earlier
- Establish trust sooner
- Shape decision criteria
- Influence purchasing decisions before competitors

---

## 9. Traditional Customer Personas Are Often Incomplete

Knowing:

- Age
- Location
- Job title

does not explain why customers buy.

Jobs To Be Done focuses on:

- Context
- Motivation
- Desired progress

rather than demographic profiles.

---

## 10. Most Courses Sell Information Instead Of Results

Customers often want:

- Templates
- Cheat Sheets
- Frameworks
- Checklists
- Systems

rather than long video courses.

People want the fastest path to an outcome.

---

## 11. Build Products Around The Desired Outcome

Ask:

> "What outcome is the customer trying to achieve?"

instead of:

> "What information can I teach?"

This mindset leads to more useful and actionable products.

---

## 12. Service Businesses Are Often The Best Starting Point

Benefits include:

- Faster revenue generation
- Direct customer interaction
- Market validation
- Better customer understanding

Service work creates the insights necessary to build better products later.

---

## 13. Audience Building Creates Multiple Business Opportunities

An audience can be monetized through:

- Courses
- Templates
- Consulting
- Memberships
- Sponsorships
- Software
- Services

An audience creates optionality.

---

## 14. Newsletter Growth Comes From Consistent Value

Key growth drivers included:

- Consistent publishing
- Educational content
- Social proof
- Word-of-mouth sharing
- Audience recommendations

---

## 15. Social Proof Accelerates Audience Growth

Trust transfers.

Recommendations from respected people often outperform traditional promotion.

---

## 16. Marketing Is About Being Less Wrong Faster

### Key Quote

> "The goal of marketing isn't to be right. It's to be less wrong faster."

Successful marketers:

- Test assumptions
- Learn quickly
- Gather customer insights
- Adjust based on evidence

Research helps marketers make better decisions by reducing uncertainty around:

- Messaging
- Positioning
- Audience targeting
- Offer creation

# Research Notes

## Most Valuable Insight

Customers do not buy because of demographics, features, or company messaging alone.

They buy because a trigger event creates a need for progress, and they hire products that help them make that progress.

Understanding those trigger events provides a significant competitive advantage.

---

## Application To B2B SaaS

B2B SaaS companies can apply Jobs To Be Done principles by focusing on:

- Customer triggers
- Buying motivations
- Desired outcomes
- Customer research
- Real user stories

Instead of leading with features and technical capabilities, SaaS companies should position products around the progress customers want to achieve.

This creates stronger positioning, more effective messaging, and better product-market fit over time.
